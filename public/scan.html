Contractor Basics

Behavioral control refers to whether you have the right to direct or control how work is performed — not just the outcome.

Do you set the contractor's work hours?

Do you direct HOW they complete their work (not just the outcome)?

Do you provide training or require specific methods?

Can you assign additional work outside the original scope without renegotiation?

Financial control looks at whether the business controls the economic aspects of the worker's job.

Does the contractor work exclusively for you?

Do you provide their primary tools or equipment?

Is their pay a fixed salary (not per-project or hourly by choice)?

Do you reimburse all of their business expenses?

Relationship type examines how the parties perceive their arrangement and whether it resembles an employment relationship.

Is there a written contract defining the relationship as independent?

Does the relationship have a defined end date or project scope?

Do you provide employee benefits (health insurance, vacation, retirement, etc.)?

Is the work they do core to your business operations?

Multi-client activity is one of the strongest indicators of true independent contractor status. A genuine contractor typically maintains an independent business.

Does the contractor actively advertise their services to other potential clients?

Have they worked with other businesses in the last 12 months?
